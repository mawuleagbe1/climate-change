About Ghana's Climate Challenge

Understanding the unique climate vulnerabilities facing Ghana

Ghana's Climate Profile

Ghana, located in West Africa along the Gulf of Guinea, experiences a tropical climate with distinct wet and dry seasons. The country is divided into three main climatic zones:

Coastal Zone

The southern coastal belt has a semi-equatorial climate with two rainy seasons (April-July and September-October) and high humidity. Average temperatures range between 24¬∞C and 30¬∞C.

Forest Zone

The middle belt has a tropical rainforest climate with significant rainfall (1,500-2,000mm annually). This region is crucial for Ghana's cocoa production and biodiversity.

Northern Zone

The northern savanna experiences a single rainy season (May-September) and is prone to droughts. Temperatures can exceed 40¬∞C during the hot season (March-April).

Why Ghana is Vulnerable

Ghana ranks among the countries most vulnerable to climate change due to several factors:

  • Economic dependence on climate-sensitive sectors: Agriculture contributes about 20% of GDP and employs over 40% of the workforce, particularly in cocoa, which is highly climate-sensitive.
  • High poverty levels: Approximately 23% of Ghanaians live in poverty, limiting their capacity to adapt to climate impacts.
  • Coastal vulnerability: About 25% of the population lives in coastal areas threatened by sea level rise and coastal erosion.
  • Energy dependence on hydropower: Hydropower accounts for about 38% of electricity generation, making the energy sector vulnerable to rainfall variability.
  • Rapid urbanization: Urban areas face increasing heat stress and flood risks due to poor drainage and planning.

Ghana's Unique Vulnerabilities

Why our nation faces disproportionate climate risks

Economic Dependence on Climate-Sensitive Sectors

Nearly 45% of Ghana's workforce is employed in agriculture, fisheries and forestry - sectors highly vulnerable to climate shifts. Our $2 billion cocoa industry, which supplies 20% of global production, faces existential threats as optimal growing regions shift due to rising temperatures and changing rainfall patterns.

Water Stress Challenges

Ghana's water resources are under dual pressures from climate change and growing demand. The Akosombo Dam, which provides 35% of our electricity, has seen reduced water levels during recent droughts. Meanwhile, saltwater intrusion is contaminating freshwater aquifers in coastal areas like the Densu Delta.

Urban Heat Amplification

Accra's rapid urbanization has created intense heat islands where temperatures can be 5-7¬∞C higher than surrounding rural areas. The combination of heat-trapping concrete, reduced green spaces, and waste heat from vehicles and industry creates dangerous conditions for vulnerable populations.

Extended droughts in northern Ghana are becoming more frequent and severe

Rainfall Variability

Annual rainfall has decreased by 20% in northern Ghana since 1950, while southern regions experience more intense downpours that overwhelm drainage systems

Coastal Threats

3 major coastal cities (Accra, Takoradi, Cape Coast) face $1.2 billion in climate-related infrastructure risks by 2050

Health Impacts

Malaria transmission zones have expanded to cover 90% of Ghana, with disease season extending by 2 months since 1990

The Global Climate Crisis

Understanding the planetary changes affecting us all

Planetary Warming Trends

The Earth's average surface temperature has risen by about 1.1¬∞C since the late 19th century, with the last decade (2011-2020) being the warmest on record. This warming is occurring at a rate unprecedented in at least the last 2,000 years. The oceans have absorbed much of this increased heat, with the top 100 meters warming by more than 0.6¬∞F (0.33¬∞C) since 1969.

In Ghana, this global trend manifests as more frequent and intense heat waves, particularly in northern regions where temperatures now regularly exceed 40¬∞C during the hot season. Nighttime temperatures are also rising, reducing the natural cooling period essential for human health and crop recovery.

Cryosphere Collapse

The Earth's frozen regions are disappearing at alarming rates. Greenland lost an average of 279 billion tons of ice per year between 1993 and 2019, while Antarctica lost about 148 billion tons annually. This ice melt contributes directly to sea level rise, which has accelerated from 1.4 mm per year throughout most of the 20th century to 3.6 mm per year from 2006-2015.

While Ghana doesn't have glaciers, these changes affect us through rising sea levels that threaten our coastal communities. The Volta Delta is particularly vulnerable, with projections suggesting up to 1,000 square kilometers could be inundated by 2100 under high emission scenarios.

Climate Justice Perspective

The unequal burden of climate impacts

Disproportionate Impacts

While Ghana contributes less than 0.1% of global greenhouse gas emissions, we rank among the top 20% of countries most vulnerable to climate impacts. This injustice reflects a global pattern where nations least responsible for climate change face the most severe consequences.

Gender Dimensions

Climate change exacerbates existing gender inequalities. Women, who constitute 70% of Ghana's agricultural workforce, face greater challenges as climate impacts reduce crop yields and water availability. Climate-related disasters also increase women's unpaid care work by an estimated 30%.

Intergenerational Equity

Ghana's youth (under 25s make up 56% of population) will inherit the consequences of today's climate decisions. Many are already leading adaptation efforts through initiatives like the Ghana Youth Climate Coalition, which has planted over 100,000 trees since 2018.

Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to the most common questions about climate change and its impacts in Ghana

What is the difference between weather and climate?

Weather refers to short-term atmospheric conditions (hours to days), while climate is the average weather over longer periods (typically 30 years or more). Climate change refers to long-term shifts in temperature, rainfall, and other atmospheric conditions.

For example, a particularly hot day in Accra is weather, but the increasing frequency of hot days over decades is climate change.

Is climate change caused by human activities?

Yes, the overwhelming scientific consensus confirms that human activities are the primary driver of current climate change. The burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas), deforestation, and industrial processes have increased greenhouse gas concentrations to levels unseen in at least 800,000 years.

In Ghana, key contributors include:

  • Energy production from fossil fuels
  • Deforestation for agriculture and logging
  • Transportation emissions
  • Waste management practices

How is climate change affecting Ghana specifically?

Ghana is experiencing several climate change impacts:

  • Temperature rise: Average temperatures have increased by 1¬∞C since 1960
  • Rainfall changes: More intense rains in the south, longer droughts in the north
  • Coastal erosion: Sea level rise of 3.4mm per year threatening communities
  • Agricultural impacts: Shifting growing seasons affecting cocoa and staple crops
  • Health risks: Increased malaria and heat-related illnesses

Which regions of Ghana are most vulnerable to climate change?

Different regions face different vulnerabilities:

  • Northern Ghana: Most vulnerable to droughts and desertification
  • Coastal areas: At risk from sea level rise and coastal erosion (e.g., Keta, Ada)
  • Urban centers: Flooding in Accra and other cities during heavy rains
  • Forest zones: Deforestation and biodiversity loss in the Ashanti and Western regions

The poor and rural populations are typically most vulnerable due to limited adaptation resources.

What is Ghana doing to address climate change?

Ghana has implemented several climate initiatives:

  • National Climate Change Policy (2013): Framework for climate action
  • Renewable Energy Master Plan: Targeting 10% renewable energy by 2030
  • REDD+ Strategy: Reducing Emissions from Deforestation
  • National Adaptation Plan: Helping communities adapt to impacts
  • Planting for Food and Jobs: Promoting climate-smart agriculture

Ghana has also committed to reducing emissions by 15-45% by 2030 under the Paris Agreement.

What can I do to help combat climate change in Ghana?

Individuals can make a difference through:

  • Energy conservation: Use energy-efficient appliances and lighting
  • Sustainable transport: Walk, bike, or use public transport when possible
  • Waste reduction: Recycle and compost organic waste
  • Tree planting: Participate in community reforestation efforts
  • Water conservation: Fix leaks and use water wisely
  • Education: Learn and share information about climate change
  • Advocacy: Support climate-friendly policies and leaders

Small actions multiplied by millions of people can create significant change.
